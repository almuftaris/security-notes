# Google Cloud Security Podcast EP266 - Code War: Nations Hack, Spy, and Shape the Digital Battlefield

**Date**: March 12, 2026
**Guest**: Allie Mellen, Author of "Code War: How Nations Hack, Spy, and Shape the Digital Battlefield" (March 2026)
**Source**: [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3I76AH4MYeQ)

## Why Write This Book Now?

Mellen argues we're at an inflection point where cyber operations are no longer a supporting element of geopolitics - they're a primary instrument of statecraft. The recent Iran-Israel cyber campaigns and Operation Epic Fury demonstrate this shift: cyber attacks aren't happening alongside military operations, they're integrated into them.

**The book's timing**: Published just weeks after the largest coordinated cyber-kinetic operation in history, making it immediately relevant to current events.

## The Cyber Warfare Landscape

### **Three Categories of State Cyber Operations**:

**1. Espionage (Intelligence Gathering)**:
- Longest-running and most common state activity
- Every major nation does this constantly
- Examples: APT groups stealing IP, monitoring communications, mapping adversary networks
- Generally accepted (if not publicly acknowledged) part of international relations
- Line gets blurry when espionage enables future attacks

**2. Influence Operations (Information Warfare)**:
- Shaping public opinion, undermining trust in institutions
- Election interference, disinformation campaigns
- Social media manipulation at scale
- Often combined with leaked data from espionage operations
- Harder to attribute, easier to deny

**3. Disruption/Destruction (Active Attacks)**:
- Wipers, ransomware, infrastructure sabotage
- Crossing into "act of war" territory
- Examples: Ukraine power grid attacks, Iranian nuclear facility sabotage
- Escalation risk is real - where's the line that triggers kinetic retaliation?

## The Attribution Problem

**Mellen's perspective**: Attribution is getting better but it's still the fundamental challenge in cyber conflict.

**Why attribution matters**:
- Can't respond appropriately if you don't know who attacked you
- Diplomatic/economic responses require confidence in attribution
- False flag operations are real (intentional misattribution)
- Private sector attributions don't carry same weight as government intelligence

**The improvement**: 
- Better telemetry and threat intelligence sharing between governments and private sector
- Operational security mistakes by threat actors
- Pattern analysis across campaigns (TTPs, infrastructure reuse)

**Still difficult**:
- State actors using criminal infrastructure for plausible deniability
- Proxy groups (state-sponsored but maintain distance)
- Time delay between attack and confident attribution

## Norms and Escalation

### **Do Cyber Norms Exist?**

Mellen argues there are emerging informal norms, but they're not codified or universally accepted:

**Somewhat accepted norms**:
- Don't attack hospitals during peacetime (violated during conflicts)
- Don't disrupt critical civilian infrastructure without warning (honored more in breach)
- Espionage is "fair game" but don't get caught

**Gray areas**:
- What constitutes "critical infrastructure"?
- Is temporary disruption different from permanent destruction?
- Where's the line between espionage and pre-positioning for attack?

**No agreement on**:
- Proportional response frameworks
- Threshold for cyber attack that justifies kinetic retaliation
- Rules of engagement during active conflicts

### **Escalation Dynamics**:

**The concern**: Cyber attacks are relatively low-cost and low-risk compared to kinetic operations, which might make states more willing to use them. But what happens when a cyber attack causes real physical harm or cascading failures?

**Example from book**: If a nation attacks another's power grid during winter, causing deaths from exposure, is that different from a missile strike? How should the victim respond?

**Mellen's take**: We're learning these boundaries in real-time through trial and error. 

Iran-Israel cyber campaigns are the clearest modern example.

## The Private Sector's Role

### **Shift in Responsibility**:

Historically: Governments handled national security, companies focused on business  
Now: Critical infrastructure is privately owned, making companies de facto participants in national defense

**Implications**:
- Private companies have intelligence on state actors that governments need
- Government increasingly relying on private sector threat intel (CrowdStrike, Mandiant, etc.)
- Companies making their own attribution calls and going public
- Blurred lines between corporate security and national security

**The tension**:
- Companies want to protect customers and maintain trust
- Governments want companies to share threat intel and sometimes stay quiet
- What happens when business interests conflict with national security?

### **The Threat Intel Ecosystem**:

**Mellen highlights**: Private threat intelligence firms have become quasi-intelligence agencies
- Tracking nation-state campaigns
- Publishing attribution assessments
- Providing governments with insights they might not have
- Sometimes more agile than traditional intelligence agencies

**Questions this raises**:
- Should private companies be making public attribution claims about nation-states?
- What's the liability if they get it wrong?
- Are we outsourcing intelligence analysis to profit-driven companies?

## Cyber as Force Multiplier vs Primary Weapon

**Mellen's framework**: Cyber started as a force multiplier (supporting kinetic operations) but is evolving into a primary weapon system.

**Force Multiplier Examples**:
- Disrupting communications before kinetic strike
- Mapping adversary networks for targeting
- Psychological operations to demoralize defenders

**Primary Weapon Examples**:
- Stuxnet (achieved strategic objective without kinetic attack)
- NotPetya (caused $10B+ in damage globally)
- Colonial Pipeline ransomware (disrupted critical infrastructure)

**The evolution**: Nation-states realizing cyber can achieve strategic goals alone, not just support other operations. This changes calculations around when and how to use it.

## The Iran-Israel Case Study

**Book's relevance**: Published just after Operation Epic Fury, so the interview naturally focused on how recent events validate Mellen's thesis.

**What the campaigns demonstrated**:
- **Integration**: Cyber and kinetic operations synchronized, not sequential
- **Scale**: Internet connectivity down to 4%, state media offline - total information warfare
- **Psychological ops**: BadeSaba app compromise (5M users getting surrender messages)
- **Retaliation certainty**: Iranian APTs activated within hours

This is the new normal for state conflicts.
- Cyber operations start before kinetic operations (shaping the battlefield)
- Continue during (disrupting C2, spreading disinformation)
- Persist after (espionage, influence operations)

Future conflicts will assume cyber operations as baseline, not optional component.

## Defensive Recommendations

### **For Organizations**:

**1. Assume you're a target** (even if you don't think you are):
- Critical infrastructure obviously targeted. Supply chain attacks make everyone relevant. Data you hold might be valuable to nation-states

**2. Invest in basics**:
- Patching, MFA, network segmentation. Advanced persistent threats persist because basics aren't done

**3. Threat intelligence matters**:
- Know which nation-state actors target your sector, understand their TTPs and priorities. Share the information with peers and government

### **For Governments**:

**1. Public-private partnerships are critical**:
- Infrastructure is privately owned, defense requires collaboration
- Intel sharing needs to flow both directions
- Regulatory frameworks that enable security without crushing business

**2. Deterrence is unclear**:
- No agreed framework for proportional cyber response
- Needs to be developed before major crisis, not during

**3. Norms development**:
- International agreements on what's off-limits
- Consequences for violations
- Verification mechanisms

## The Book's Core Argument

**Mellen's thesis**: We're in a "Code War" era where software, networks, and data are the battlefield. Nations that don't recognize this and adapt their security posture, defense strategies, and international relationships accordingly will lose.

**Why "Code War" not "Cyber War"**:
- It's about the code running everything, not just "cyber" as separate domain
- Software supply chain is the battlefield
- Every organization is infrastructure now (cloud, SaaS dependencies)
- The war is constant, not episodic
